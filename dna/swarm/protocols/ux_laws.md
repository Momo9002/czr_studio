# Essential UX & UI Laws

World-class interfaces are governed by universal laws of human-computer interaction. Apply these continuously during structure, design, and development phases.

## 1. Hick’s Law
*The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices.*
- **Application:** Minimize navigation links. Don't overwhelm the user with CTAs. Keep the primary action obvious and isolate secondary actions.

## 2. Fitts’s Law
*The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target.*
- **Application:** Make call-to-action (CTA) buttons large, clickable, and easy to reach (especially on mobile). Add generous padding to links.

## 3. Miller’s Law
*The average person can only keep 7 (plus or minus 2) items in their working memory.*
- **Application:** Chunk information into smaller groups. Limit features/benefits lists to 3-5 items. 

## 4. The Von Restorff Effect (Isolation Effect)
*When multiple similar objects are present, the one that differs from the rest is most likely to be remembered.*
- **Application:** The primary CTA should visually stand out from all other elements on the page (e.g., a solid high-contrast button vs. outlined secondary buttons).

## 5. Law of Proximity
*Objects that are near, or proximate to each other, tend to be grouped together.*
- **Application:** Use whitespace (negative space) intentionally to group related content (like a heading, paragraph, and button forming a single "card" or concept).

## 6. Law of Prägnanz
*People will perceive and interpret ambiguous or complex images as the simplest form possible.*
- **Application:** Prioritize clean, grid-based, familiar layouts over overly complex UI patterns that users have to "figure out."
